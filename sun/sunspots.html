The Fascinating History of Sunspots

The Dawn of a New Era

This story begins in the Netherlands on October 2, 1608, when Hans Lipperhey, a German optician naturalized in the Netherlands, filed a patent for his design of the telescope—an instrument that would soon spread across Europe, revolutionizing astronomical observation and paving the way for numerous sensational discoveries. Two years later, in 1610, Thomas Harriot and Galileo Galilei began the scientific study of the Sun using the telescope, observing sunspots for the first time. Thomas Harriot, an English astronomer, mathematician, and ethnographer, made his first observations of sunspots in December 1610 but never published his findings. Galileo Galilei, the renowned astronomer, philosopher, mathematician, and father of modern science, acquired one of the first telescopes, made significant improvements to it, and dedicated himself to observing celestial bodies. He began a systematic study of the Sun and its sunspots, laying the foundation for modern solar astronomy.

Meanwhile, on June 13, 1611, Johannes Fabricius published De Maculis in Sole observatis, et apparente earum cum Sole conversione Narratio (Narration on the Spots Observed on the Sun and Their Apparent Rotation with the Sun), the first official document discussing sunspots. Fabricius, an astronomer born in Lower Saxony in 1587, studied in Leiden, the Netherlands, where he became acquainted with the earliest telescopes. In March 1611, back in Osteel, Germany, he began observing the Sun through a telescope with the assistance of his father. To his surprise, he noticed that the Sun was spotted. Observations made over the following months allowed him to understand that these spots were not fixed or permanent. They moved across the solar surface, disappearing from one side and reappearing on the other, revealing that our star rotates on its own axis.

Christoph Scheiner vs Galileo Galilei

The following years, 1612 and 1613, proved to be crucial. In January 1612, the Jesuit Christoph Scheiner published Tres Epistolae de Maculis Solaribus Scriptae ad Marcum Welserum (“Three Letters on Solar Spots Written to Marc Welser”). A year later, in Istoria e dimostrazioni intorno alle macchie solari (“History and Demonstrations Concerning Sunspots”), Galileo Galilei published letters announcing the discovery of dark spots on the Sun’s surface. These two works are fundamental, yet they offered very different explanations for the sunspots observed. Scheiner, a Jesuit faithful to the prevailing theories about the Sun’s immutability, interpreted the spots as shadows cast by small satellite bodies orbiting very close to the Sun—though he would later revise his views in his monumental Rosa Ursina (1630). Galileo, on the other hand, quickly understood that the spots were real features of the Sun itself, variable and present on or near its surface, even if he could not fully explain them. For the Pisan scientist, these were flat clouds clinging to the Sun’s surface, and by observing their constant movement, he deduced that the Sun must rotate on its axis at a uniform speed.

Before the Advent of the Telescope

But let’s take a step back: what was the situation before 1600?

The earliest known observation of sunspots is attributed to Theophrastus, a student of Aristotle, and dates back to the 4th century BCE. Numerous sunspot observations were also recorded in Chinese, Japanese, and Korean annals. In Western literature, however, references to such observations are rare, likely due to the influence of Aristotelian prejudice—later adopted by Christian theology—regarding an immutable and incorruptible celestial world. Interestingly, almost all of the few sunspot observations made in the West before the introduction of the telescope were interpreted as the transits of Venus, Mercury, or other supposed planets, called “inframercurial,” thought to exist between Mercury and the Sun.

Before the advent of the telescope, the years 1128 and 1129 provide the most significant evidence of sunspot observations in both the East and the West. Although Chinese astronomers had begun recording solar activity as early as 800 BCE, neither they nor Korean astronomers produced drawings, limiting themselves to simple textual descriptions of the events. The first known drawing of sunspots appears in The Chronicle of John of Worcester from 1128. This medieval chronicle covers the period up to 1140 and records numerous astronomical events, including auroras, comets, meteor showers, as well as solar and lunar eclipses.

But what happens in these years?

On December 8, 1128, John of Worcester recorded in his chronicles the appearance of sunspots. Five days later, on the night of December 13, at Sogdo (modern-day Kaesong) in Korea, astronomers observed a red vapor “rising and filling the sky” from northwest to southwest. This event corresponds to an aurora borealis, appearing shortly after the sunspots noted by Worcester.

Five days is roughly the estimated average delay between the appearance of a large sunspot group near the center of the Sun and the occurrence of auroras at relatively low latitudes. On March 22, 1129, Chinese astronomers recorded a “black spot” on the Sun’s surface, which disappeared on April 14. This may have been one of the sunspots observed by Worcester 104 days earlier, on the other side of the world.

The Fall of Aristotelian Dogmas

Observing sunspots through the telescope challenged the long-standing notion of the Sun as a perfect celestial body and, together with other astronomical observations — such as the supernovae of 1572 and 1604 — called into question the immutability of the heavens. These ideas had been upheld by Aristotelians, who believed that no change could occur in the skies, composed solely of the perfect substance, the quintessence. Such observations ultimately undermined the core dogmas of Aristotelian cosmology.

The Sun and Sunspots in the New Millennium

More than four hundred years have passed since the first telescopic observations, and tremendous progress has been made. Our understanding of the Sun and sunspots has deepened significantly, thanks both to advances in astrophysics and to the use of cutting-edge satellites and telescopes.

Today we know that sunspots are the most visible manifestation of the solar magnetic field in the Sun’s visible atmosphere, and their origin is linked to an active dynamo in the solar convection zone. A well-developed sunspot consists of two parts: a central, darker, rounded region called the umbra, and the surrounding penumbra, made up of radial filaments. The umbra hosts magnetic fields thousands of times stronger than Earth’s magnetic field (typically 3,000–4,000 gauss). Sunspots appear dark only in contrast to the much brighter surrounding regions, with the umbra at their center being cooler and emitting less light than the photospheric surface. Sunspots usually appear in pairs with opposite magnetic polarity and can last from less than a day to several months.
